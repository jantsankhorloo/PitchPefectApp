About Me

I current work as a Senior Performance Architect for Netflix, working on very large scale performance issues for one of the largest application deployments in the world - all running in Amazon AWS. I get to write a lot of nifty tools for analyzing performance data and keep my Linux skills sharp with the size of our Linux fleet. People talk about "web scale", I work on "Netflix scale".

Prior to Netflix I worked for Okta as Lead Performance Engineer. As Okta was a start-up when I joined, I did a very broad array of engineering tasks. In addition to working on a variety of performance issues, I refactored code, rewrote the Ant build system into Maven, and spent much time working on software architecture and engineering discipline.

Somewhere in here I took a 6 month sabbatical, sailing our Privilege 39 catamaran Jane 'O to the Sea of Cortez in Mexico and back - spending weeks at a time completely unplugged while anchored in the remote islands North of La Paz with no internet, no cell service, and not enough electricity to run power-hungry devices like computers. You can read about our trip at our blog here. If you ever get a chance to unplug for an extended period of time, you should do it. You'll come back a different person.

I've also worked at Rearden Commerce, Amdocs (via DST Innovis acquisition, via US Computer Services acquisition, which was formerly Cable Data), and IBM - all in the software engineering field at some level, with a mix of performance engineering, systems administration, software development, and networking.

I'm proud about the fact that my name isn't that uncommon - and as a result, a search for my full name returns a ton of results that are not me. My favorites are the obituaries - if any recruiters are reading this - one of those is me, I'm dead. Sorry I can't come work for you.

Linux and Open Source

Ever since my first encounter with UNIX in the early-80's, I've been a hard-core UNIX user, working with everything from Edition 7, Ultrix, SVR4, AIX, IRIX, Digital Unix, HPUX, Solaris, FreeBSD, Linux, and others I've long since forgotten.

Since the early 2000's I've run Linux on my desktop at home and at work (sometimes violating corporiate policies). I currently run straight Debian systems - Debian Wheezy is fantastic - and I use the i3 tiling window manager. I have a few systems running Debian Jessie (Testing) as well - and yes, they are quite happily running with systemd. In the end, the debate about systemd is silly and most of the people who complain about it have never actually tried it - relying on FUD from people with an axe to grind.

Ubuntu has done a good marketing job, but they are on the wrong path. Projects like Linux Mint have done a decent job of de-Ubuntu'ing it - but when you really sit right down and think about it, why not just use base Debian? Start without the crap to begin with. It works - really! It's great!

I've contributed to many open source projects over the years and consider my career to be largely enabled by it's existence (even though I've worked at companies that have shunned it). I abhor software patents, and look forward to the day when closed, litigious, ecosystems like Microsoft, Oracle, and Apple are dead.

I mentioned companies I worked for that were anti-open source. The worst of these was DST, where a lawyer once told me "the only reason someone would give away software for free is because they stole it from someone else". Yes, the corporate lawyer actually truly believed that, and couldn't understand software as a hobby, or the altruistic nature of open source driving technology and the industry forward. I guess when you are in a profession with zero forward facing vision and where you bill the customer for every staple... well you just can't get it.
